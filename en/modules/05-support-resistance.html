MODULE 05

Support / Resistance & Fibonacci

The levels where money changes hands

Support and resistance: zones, not lines

First thing to remember — and the one 90% of beginners miss — a support or resistance is a zone, not a pixel-perfect line. When you draw an S/R at exactly 2045.00, you'll get stopped out because price will touch 2044.80 or 2045.40. The reality is that levels are fuzzy: think in terms of zones of 5 to 15 dollars on XAUUSD depending on the timeframe.

A support is a zone where historically buyers have stepped in aggressively. A resistance is the opposite. These zones exist because traders remember them, have placed orders there, or took losses they want to recover.

How to identify S/R

  • Swing highs / swing lows: past pivot points. The more a level has been tested, the more significant it is (up to a point — see below).
  • Multiple rejection zones: if price has touched a zone 3-4 times and bounced each time, it's a strong level.
  • Round numbers (psychological levels): on XAUUSD, levels in hundreds (2000, 2100, 2200) and fifties (2050, 2150) are huge. Humans love round numbers, and algos do too (because they're programmed by humans).
  • Former consolidation zones: horizontal ranges often become S/R zones once broken.
The paradox of the over-tested level
A level tested 2-3 times is strong. A level tested 7 times will probably break. Why? Because with each test, the available liquidity at that level decreases. Nobody is left to defend it, and one decent push breaks through. It's counterintuitive but essential.

Polarity: broken resistance = support

When a resistance is cleanly broken (close above, not just a wick), it becomes a support on subsequent retests. And vice versa for a broken support. This is one of the most reliable patterns in trading.

XAUUSD example: the 2000 level was a massive resistance for years. Once broken in 2023, it became a major support that price came back to multiple times. Classic.

Dynamic S/R: moving averages and VWAP

Support/resistance levels aren't only horizontal. Some evolve with price:

  • EMA 20: intraday, it's the "reactive" average — in a trend, price often retests it then continues.
  • EMA 50: swing trading, often respected on H4 and D1.
  • EMA 200: the "line in the sand." Above it = long-term bullish bias, below = bearish. Watched by a massive number of traders, making it self-fulfilling.
  • VWAP (Volume Weighted Average Price): the volume-weighted average price since the session open. Used heavily by institutional desks. On XAUUSD, the daily VWAP acts like a magnet.

Multi-timeframe: hierarchy matters

Not all levels are created equal. A level drawn on the Daily is far stronger than a level drawn on the 15-minute chart. The hierarchy:

Monthly > Weekly > Daily > H4 > H1 > M15 > M5

When a D1 level coincides with an H4 level and an H1 EMA 200, you have three signals aligned on the same price. That's what we call confluence.

Confluence: the realistic holy grail

Confluence is when multiple independent signals point to the same level. The more signals you have, the more reliable the setup. Perfect example on XAUUSD:

  • D1 support at 2050
  • H4 EMA 50 passing at 2048
  • Fib 61.8% of the last impulse at 2051
  • Round psychological number 2050
  • Historical rejection zone (2 previous bounces)

That's a level you wait for. You don't chase price — you let it come to you.

How to draw: wicks or bodies?

The eternal debate. The honest answer: both. Draw a zone that encompasses both the bodies AND the wicks of previous tests. A properly drawn S/R looks like a rectangle, not a line. Wicks define the outer zone (stop loss), bodies define the inner zone (entry).

Fibonacci retracement

Fibonacci is a mathematical sequence whose certain ratios appear strangely often in finance. The important ratios for trading:

  • 23.6%: shallow pullback, strong trend
  • 38.2%: moderate pullback, common in a healthy trend
  • 50%: this is not a true Fibonacci ratio, but psychologically huge (half the move)
  • 61.8%: the "golden ratio," the most respected level, often the last chance for a pullback before reversal
  • 78.6%: deep pullback, entering the "invalidation" zone

How to draw a fib

In an uptrend: draw from the swing low to the swing high of the last impulse. The retracement levels appear between the two. In a downtrend: the reverse (swing high to swing low).

The 61.8% - 78.6% zone is called OTE (Optimal Trade Entry) in SMC / ICT. It's the zone where institutions often fill their remaining orders before pushing price in the direction of the trend.

Diagram: Fibonacci retracement

Classic Fibonacci retracement: bullish impulse, pullback finding its low in the OTE zone (61.8-78.6%), then trend resumption.

Fibonacci extension: for take-profit targets

Extensions are used to project targets (Take Profit) beyond the initial swing high. The key levels:

  • 127.2%: first target, often reached
  • 161.8%: golden extension, primary target
  • 200% / 261.8%: extended targets for large moves
Watch out for Fibonacci overfitting
The classic trap: you draw 15 fibs on 15 different swings until you find the one that "fits" the pullback low, then you say "see, Fibonacci works!" That's curve fitting, and it's useless. Rule: choose your swing before the pullback begins, not after. One fib at a time, drawn on the most obvious impulse of the timeframe you're trading.

Practical XAUUSD example

Take the round levels on Gold: 1900, 1950, 2000, 2050, 2100, 2150, 2200... Draw them on your Daily chart. You'll see they act as magnets and barriers with an almost embarrassing regularity. Combine them with major Daily swings, and you already have 80% of the levels you need to trade. Add a fib on the last major D1 impulse, and you're ready.
