MODULE 04

Pure Price Action

Reading naked price, without indicators — the Al Brooks approach

What is Price Action?

Price Action is the art of reading price as it is, without indicators, without RSI, without MACD, without flashing colored lines. Just candles, levels, and structure. You watch what buyers and sellers are doing right now, and you try to infer what they'll probably do in the next few candles.

This approach was popularized (and formalized to the point of obsession) by Al Brooks, an American trader whose books became the bible of price action trading. Brace yourself: it's dense, technical, and it will take you hundreds of hours before you start seeing what he sees in half a second.

Why it works

Price contains all the information. An indicator is just a mathematical transformation of price — by definition, it lags. RSI tells you a move has already happened; the candle shows it happening in real time. The best institutional traders barely look at indicators: they read the tape and the levels.

The 3 market states

At any given moment, the market is in one of these three states. Not four, not two. Three.

  • Trend (bull or bear): price makes higher highs and higher lows (bull) or lower highs and lower lows (bear). Pullbacks are short, impulses are long.
  • Range (trading range): price oscillates between a support and a resistance. Roughly 70-80% of the time, the market is in a range — remember that.
  • Breakout: a fast and violent transition — price exits a range or a consolidation. Often short-lived but this is where most of a trend follower's profit comes from.

Strong trend vs weak trend

Not all trends are created equal. A strong trend, you can trade aggressively. A weak trend will stop you out five times before anything moves.

Signs of a strong trend:

  • Full-bodied candles (large bodies) in the direction of the trend
  • Short wicks on the opposite side
  • Short pullbacks (2-4 candles max) and shallow (less than 38% of the previous leg)
  • Little overlap between candles (low overlap)
  • It breaks through S/R levels without hesitation

Signs of a weak trend:

  • Candles with small bodies and large wicks on both sides
  • Deep pullbacks (50-70%) and lengthy
  • Lots of overlap, price is grinding
  • Breakouts frequently fail (fakeouts)

Al Brooks' Signal Bars

A signal bar is the candle that gives you the entry trigger. You never enter on the signal bar itself: you wait for the next candle to break its high (for a long) or its low (for a short). This filters out a huge number of false signals.

Pin bar (rejection bar)

A candle with a long wick on one side and a small body on the opposite side. The wick shows that one side tried to push and got rejected. It's the most famous signal bar, and also the trickiest because everyone sees it.

Inside bar

A candle entirely contained within the previous one (lower high, higher low). Signals a pause, a consolidation. Traded on the breakout of the "mother bar."

Engulfing

A candle whose body completely engulfs the body of the previous one, in the opposite direction. Signals a short-term shift in momentum.

BO bar (breakout bar)

A large full-bodied candle that breaks a key level with conviction. This is often your best signal bar in a strong trend.

The trap of isolated signal bars

A pin bar in the middle of nowhere is worth nothing. A pin bar on an H4 support with fib confluence + a round number, after a pullback in a D1 bullish trend — that's worth a lot. Context is 80% of the signal. The signal bar is just the cherry on top.

Pullbacks: H1/H2, L1/L2

Brooks has a precise nomenclature for pullbacks within a trend. In an uptrend:

  • H1: the first candle that makes a higher high after a pullback of 2+ bearish candles. It's the first buy signal on the pullback.
  • H2: if H1 fails and the pullback continues, the second attempt. Statistically more reliable than H1 in a moderate trend.

In a downtrend, it's L1 and L2 (symmetrical). In practice, H2/L2 often corresponds to a Fibonacci retracement around 50-61.8%.

"Always in long" / "Always in short"

A Brooks concept: at any given moment, you must have a directional bias. If you had to be in a position right now, which direction would it be? It's not "neither long nor short." It's always one or the other, even in a range (the bias then flips at each extreme). It forces you to have an opinion and update it in real time.

Classic traps

  • Fake reversal bars: a pin bar that looks perfect but isn't followed by any reversal. Without context, it's useless.
  • Failed breakouts: price breaks a level, you enter on the breakout, then it comes back into the range. Very common on XAUUSD during quiet Asian sessions.
  • Bull traps / Bear traps: a breakout followed by a violent reversal in the opposite direction. Classic institutional trap to sweep retail stop losses.

Classic sequence: 3 impulse candles, 3-candle pullback, pin bar on support, bullish confirmation, entry on the break of the pin bar's high.

Volume and context

On Forex and CFDs, volume is imperfect (no centralized volume). But on Gold futures (GC) you have it. Simple rule: a breakout with volume = serious; a breakout without volume = suspicious. On XAUUSD spot, use your broker's tick volume as a proxy — imperfect but usable.

Let's be honest: Price Action is hard

Reading price action correctly requires thousands of hours of chart observation. Al Brooks himself says it: expect 3 to 5 years to become competent. There is no shortcut, no "magic pattern," no YouTube video that will change everything. It's pattern recognition developed through massive exposure. If you're looking for something quick, move on.

Going further

The reference book: "Reading Price Charts Bar by Bar" by Al Brooks (2009). Plan to read it 2 or 3 times. It's dry, repetitive, but comprehensive. He also published a more recent trilogy (Trends, Trading Ranges, Reversals) that's even more detailed.
