MODULE 11

Psychology & cognitive biases

After 5 years without profit, it's almost always here that things go wrong. Not in the strategy.

You don't trade the market, you trade your psychology

This quote from Mark Douglas is the most uncomfortable truth of trading. The market doesn't know you, doesn't hold a grudge against you, and isn't trying to trap you. It's you who looks for setups that don't exist, you who moves your SL, you who doubles down after a loss. The market is a ruthless mirror. And the final boss of trading is you.

1. The silent killers

These behaviors liquidate more accounts than crashes. You've already done all of them. What matters is recognizing them before they become chronic.

  • FOMO (Fear Of Missing Out): the move has already started, you weren't in it, and you jump in at the worst possible spot out of fear of missing it. Result: entry at the top, SL hit 10 minutes later.
  • Revenge trading: you just lost, you want to "make it back immediately". You double the size, you force a trade that doesn't exist. Classic ruin.
  • Overtrading: you confuse activity with productivity. 10 mediocre trades are worth less than a single A+. But you're bored, so you click.
  • Hesitation: you finally have your A+ setup, but you just lost, so you don't take it. Obviously, that's the one that would have done +5R.
  • Moving stops: "just this once, I'll move the SL 20 pips further". This single sentence has destroyed more accounts than all financial crises combined.
The boredom test

If you take a trade because you're bored, it's not a trade, it's an expensive hobby. Very expensive. For your broker.

2. Cognitive biases — you're wired to lose

Daniel Kahneman (Thinking, Fast and Slow) showed that the human brain is not designed for trading. It's designed to survive in the savanna, not to execute a probabilistic edge. Here are the biases that kill you:

  • Loss aversion: the pain of a loss is about 2 times stronger than the pleasure of an equivalent gain. Result: you cut winners early (to lock in the pleasure) and let losers run (to avoid feeling the pain). Exactly the opposite of what you should do.
  • Confirmation bias: once in a position, you only see signals that confirm your trade. All contrary signals become invisible.
  • Sunk cost fallacy: "I've already lost $300 on this trade, I can't exit now, that would mean accepting the loss". But the market doesn't care about what you've already lost.
  • Recency bias: you overweight your last 5 trades compared to the previous 200. A recent bad streak pushes you to change strategy even though nothing has changed statistically.
  • Disposition effect (Shefrin & Statman, 1985): an academic study proving that retail traders systematically cut their winners too early and let their losers run. The exact opposite of profitable behavior.
  • Overconfidence (Barber & Odean): studies show a negative correlation between self-reported confidence and actual performance. The more you think you're good, the more you trade, the more you lose.

You can't "defeat" these biases. They're hard-coded in your brain. You can only build a system that doesn't depend on their absence: written rules, mechanical execution, journal, mandatory breaks.

3. Probabilistic mindset — Mark Douglas

Mark Douglas, in Trading in the Zone, states the 5 fundamental truths that you must internalize. Not "understand intellectually". Internalize, like a religion.

  1. Anything can happen. Even the perfect setup can fail. Even the worst trade can win. No certainty, ever.
  2. You don't need to know what's going to happen to make money. You need a series of trades executed according to a probabilistic edge.
  3. There is a random distribution between winners and losers for any edge. Two identical trades will produce two different results. Normal.
  4. An edge is nothing more than an indication of a higher probability of one outcome over another. Not a guarantee. Never.
  5. Every moment in the market is unique. What you saw yesterday won't repeat identically. Treat each trade as a new bet.

When you've truly internalized these 5 points, you'll stop fearing a loss (it's part of the game) and stop being euphoric on a win (it's just a favorable draw in your distribution). You'll become emotionally neutral. And that's when you can start becoming profitable.

Think in R, not in $

Convert everything to R (multiples of your risk) instead of dollars. "-1R" hurts infinitely less than "-$150". Your brain doesn't trigger loss aversion on an abstract unit. It's a free psychological hack that changes everything.

4. Mental routine — before, during, after

Pro traders have a routine. Not because they're obsessive, but because routine short-circuits emotional decisions.

Before the session

  • Review the day's plan: pairs, key levels, scenarios.
  • Emotional self-assessment: rate 1-10. If < 6, you don't trade.
  • If/then scenarios: "if price reaches 2050 and rejects with a wick, then I go long. Otherwise, I do nothing."
  • Breathing: 5 minutes, nothing more.

During the trade

  • You breathe.
  • You follow the written plan. Not the one in your head.
  • You look at R, not at $.
  • If a strong emotion appears (fear, euphoria, anger), you stand up for 2 minutes.

After the trade

  • Immediate journal. Screenshot. Emotion felt. Plan adherence (yes/no). Lesson in 1 sentence.
  • No "cold" debrief on the weekend: you'll have forgotten. It's immediate or never.

5. Journal — non-negotiable

Brett Steenbarger, the most cited trading psychologist on Wall Street, is clear: without a journal, no progress. You can trade for 10 years, if you don't document, you'll repeat the same mistakes for 10 years straight. Your brain doesn't retain losses (loss aversion), it erases them. The journal is your external memory.

What you record for each trade:

  • Setup taken (exact pattern name)
  • Entry, SL, TPs, size, risk in R
  • Screenshot before + screenshot after
  • Emotion at the time of entry (rate 1-10)
  • Plan adherence (Y/N) — the most important metric
  • Lesson in 1 sentence

Use the built-in journal. A week without journaling = a wasted week.

The king metric

The most important number in your journal is not your PnL. It's the % of trades where you followed your plan. Aim for 90%+. The PnL will follow mechanically.

6. Anti-tilt rules

Tilt (destructive emotional state after a loss, a term borrowed from poker) is the #1 cause of catastrophes. Protocol:

  • After 2 consecutive losses → mandatory 30-minute break. Step away from the screen.
  • After 3 losses → done for the day. No negotiating with yourself.
  • Tired, angry, sick, alcohol → you don't trade. Zero exceptions.
  • Intense personal event (argument, work stress, personal news) → no trading that day.
The moment you're certain "it'll come back"

That very precise moment when you tell yourself "I feel like the next trade is the one, I'll double the size to recover" — that's THE moment you must close the platform. This feeling of certainty is the ultimate alarm signal. It announces the loss that will kill your account.

7. Essential reading

  • Mark Douglas — Trading in the Zone: the probabilistic mindset. Read it 3 times.
  • Brett Steenbarger — The Daily Trading Coach: 101 practical lessons to work on your psychology.
  • Van Tharp — Trade Your Way to Financial Freedom: expectancy, position sizing, psychology. The complete manual.
  • Jack Schwager — Market Wizards (the series): interviews with the world's best traders. Universal lessons.
  • Daniel Kahneman — Thinking, Fast and Slow: not about trading, but fundamental to understanding your own biases.

8. Summary

Your strategy is not the problem. Your mind is. Build a system (rules, journal, breaks) that makes you emotionally irrelevant. Treat each trade as a draw in a probabilistic distribution. Think in R. Document everything. Accept losses. Beware of certainty. Read Douglas. And repeat.
