MODULE 07

Smart Money Concepts (SMC / ICT)

The "smart money" vocabulary — useful, but not magic

⚠️ Read this BEFORE diving into SMC

Smart Money Concepts (SMC), derived from ICT's (Michael Huddleston) work, has become a massive trend in the retail trading community. Let's be honest from the start:

  • No independent quantitative study proves that SMC generates a statistical edge superior to chance on rigorous, reproducible backtests.
  • Massive hindsight bias: identifying an Order Block or a Fair Value Gap (FVG) after the fact is trivial. In real time, on the candle currently forming, it is radically harder.
  • The ecosystem is polluted by "gurus" selling courses for $1,500-$3,000 by recycling ICT content available for free on YouTube.
  • ICT himself is controversial (personality, past promises), but his free educational content remains generally solid technically.

Honest conclusion: use SMC as a structured vocabulary to read the market, not as a magic system. It is a reading tool, not a crystal ball. This module teaches you the concepts seriously — it is up to you to test and statistically validate them before committing capital.

1. Liquidity: the fuel of the market

The central notion of SMC/ICT is that the market is not driven by mathematical indicators, but by the hunt for Liquidity. Liquidity refers to zones where pending orders accumulate — mainly Stop Loss orders from other traders. Large players (banks, market makers) need counterparty to fill their massive positions: they therefore push price toward these zones to trigger stops.

  • Buy-side Liquidity: located above swing highs. This is where the Stop Loss orders of sellers (shorts) and the buy stops of breakout buyers sit. When price breaks these highs, a cascade of buy orders triggers.
  • Sell-side Liquidity: located below swing lows. This is where the stops of buyers (longs) and the sell stops of breakout sellers sit.
  • Equal highs / equal lows: two or three highs (or lows) at the same level = obvious Liquidity, therefore a priority target for a sweep.

The key concept: "smart money" hunts these stops before reversing. This is the liquidity sweep (or stop hunt): a rapid move that pierces the level, triggers stops, then immediately comes back. If you have ever been stopped out just before price went in your original direction — congratulations, you were the Liquidity.

Diagram: buy-side Liquidity above the swing high. The sweep briefly pierces through, then price rejects.

2. Order Block (OB)

An Order Block is the last candle opposite to the impulsive move that follows, just before that move breaks structure.

  • Bullish OB: the last bearish candle before a bullish rally that produces a bullish Break of Structure.
  • Bearish OB: the last bullish candle before a bearish drop that breaks a swing low.

A valid OB meets 3 strict criteria:

  1. The move that follows must be impulsive (large candles, few opposing wicks).
  2. The displacement must create a Fair Value Gap (FVG) (visible imbalance).
  3. The OB is ideally aligned with a prior liquidity grab (sweep of a high/low).

The Breaker Block is an OB that was invalidated (price passed through it), then retested from the other side. A broken bearish OB becomes a bullish breaker.

💡 How to distinguish a real OB from a fake one?

Simple rule: if you draw an OB and there is no FVG in the following candles, it is not a real OB. The "displacement" is the key. Without impulse, without imbalance, without a BOS behind it — it is just another candle.

3. Fair Value Gap (FVG) / Imbalance

A Fair Value Gap (FVG) is a price inefficiency visible on a 3-consecutive-candle pattern. The technical definition:

  • Bullish FVG: the high of candle 1 does not touch the low of candle 3. The space between is the gap.
  • Bearish FVG: the low of candle 1 does not touch the high of candle 3.

The SMC hypothesis: the market tends to come back and "fill" these gaps, because they represent an imbalance between buyers and sellers. In practice, many FVGs get filled, many never do — it is a tendency, not a law.

Diagram: Bullish FVG — the high of candle 1 is below the low of candle 3. The orange zone is the imbalance.

4. BOS and CHoCH — recap

Recap from module 6: Break of Structure (BOS) = continuation (one more HH in an uptrend). Change of Character (CHoCH) = first sign of reversal (an LL in an uptrend).

Market Structure Shift (MSS) is a stricter version of CHoCH: it requires an impulsive displacement (with FVG) to validate the reversal. A soft CHoCH without impulse is often a trap.

5. Premium / Discount — the equilibrium

Divide a range (or an impulsive leg) in two at the 50% level. This 50% is the equilibrium.

  • Premium (above 50%) = sell zone if bearish bias.
  • Discount (below 50%) = buy zone if bullish bias.

This is directly derived from Fibonacci. The idea: you never buy in Premium and you never sell in Discount. Simple but disciplined.

6. Optimal Trade Entry (OTE)

The OTE is the Fibonacci 61.8% – 79% zone of an impulsive leg. Combined with an OB and/or an FVG in the Discount zone (for a long) or Premium zone (for a short), it defines an entry point with reduced risk and high reward.

7. Inducement (IDM)

Inducement is an intermediate "fake" Liquidity, placed intentionally (in the SMC narrative) to trap impatient traders. Concretely: a mini-swing taken just before the main OB. If you enter on the first pullback, you get trapped; the real move starts after the IDM is taken.

8. Killzones — ICT time windows

ICT asserts that the market moves primarily within certain windows. For Gold, these Killzones are particularly relevant because they coincide with high-volume sessions:

  • London Killzone: 02:00–05:00 New York time (07:00–10:00 UTC).
  • NY Killzone: 08:30–11:00 NY (13:30–16:00 UTC).
  • Silver Bullet: 10:00–11:00 NY (15:00–16:00 UTC) — a one-hour window closely watched by ICT traders.

9. Legitimate criticisms — be honest with yourself

🔍 What course sellers will never tell you
  • Non-falsifiability: if an SMC trade fails, you will always be told "that was not the real OB" or "the correct POI was a bit lower." A concept that can never be invalidated is not scientific.
  • Subjectivity: you can draw 5 "valid" OBs on any chart looking in hindsight. Two SMC traders will rarely see the same setup.
  • No quantitative proof: no robust independent backtest shows an SMC edge above chance after transaction costs.
  • But: as a structured reading framework, it is useful. It forces you to look at Liquidity, structure, and context rather than lagging indicators.

10. How to use it CORRECTLY

  1. Always combine with market structure (module 6).
  2. Always combine with strict money management (module 9).
  3. Backtest on 200+ trades before risking a single real dollar.
  4. Keep a trading journal to identify what ACTUALLY works for YOU — not what some YouTuber says.
  5. Never pay $2,000 for an SMC course. Everything is free on the official ICT channel.
🥇 Key takeaway

SMC is a vocabulary, not a system. Liquidity, Order Block, Fair Value Gap (FVG), CHoCH, Optimal Trade Entry (OTE) — these are market-reading concepts that, combined with rigorous discipline, can structure your analysis. But without backtesting and money management, it is just decoration on a chart.
